Concept of Climate Change

Climate Change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns as well as temporal conditions. Such shifts can be natural, due to changes in the sun’s activity or large volcanic eruptions. But since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver and cause of climate change, primarily due to the burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil and gas.

Burning fossil fuels generates greenhouse gas emissions that acts like a blanket wrapped around the Earth that traps the sun’s heat into the atmosphere which causes the raising of temperatures.

The main greenhouse gases that are causing climate change include carbon dioxide and methane. These gases come from using gasoline for driving a car or coal for heating a building, for example. Clearing land and cutting down forests can also release carbon dioxide that will lead to the decreasing of the ozone layer. Agriculture, oil and gas operations are the major sources of methane emissions. Energy, industry, transport, buildings, agriculture and land use are among the main sectors causing greenhouse gases.

Causes and effects of Climate Change

Causes of Climate Change

Fossil Fuels

Coal, Oil and Gas are by far the largest contributor to global climate change, accounting for over 75 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions and nearly 90 percent of all carbon dioxide emissions.

As greenhouse gas emissions blanket the Earth, they trap the sun’s heat. This leads to global warming and climate change. The world is now warming faster than at any point in recorded history due to a lot of carbon emissions that are being produced. Warmer temperatures over time are changing weather patterns and disrupting the usual balance of nature. This poses many risks to human beings and all other forms of life on Earth.

Generating Power

Generating electricity and heat by burning fossil fuels causes a large chunk of global emissions. Most electricity is still generated by burning coal, oil, or gas, which produces carbon dioxide and nitrous oxide that is responsible for the thinning of the ozone layer – powerful greenhouse gases that blanket the Earth and trap the sun’s heat.

Manufcaturing Goods

Manufacturing and industry produce emissions, mostly from burning fossil fuels to produce energy for making things like cement, iron, steel, electronics, plastics, clothes, and other goods. Mining and other industrial processes also release gases, as does the construction industry. Machines used in the manufacturing process often run on coal, oil, or gas; and some materials, like plastics, are made from chemicals sourced from fossil fuels.

Illegal Logging

Cutting down forests to create farms or pastures, or for other reasons, causes emissions, since trees, when they are cut, release the carbon they have been storing. Each year approximately 12 million hectares of forest are destroyed because of deforestation. Since forests absorb carbon dioxide, destroying them also limits nature’s ability to keep emissions out of the atmosphere. Deforestation, together with agriculture and other land use changes, is responsible for roughly a quarter of global greenhouse gas emissions.

Effects of Climate Change

Hotter temperatures

As greenhouse gas concentrations rise, so does the global surface heat temperature. The last decade, 2011-2020, is the warmest on record. Since the 1980s, each decade has been warmer than the previous one. Nearly all land areas are being seen as hot days and heat waves due the changes of the temperature. Higher temperatures increase heat-related illnesses and make working outdoors more difficult that affects our lifestyle.

More severe storms

Destructive storms have become more intense and more frequent in many regions. As temperatures rise, more moisture evaporates, which exacerbates extreme rainfall and flooding, causing more destructive storms. The frequency and extent of tropical storms is also affected by the warming ocean. Cyclones, hurricanes, and typhoons feed on warm waters at the ocean surface.

A warming, rising ocean

The ocean soaks up most of the heat from global warming. The rate at which the ocean is warming strongly increased over the past two decades, across all depths of the ocean. As the ocean warms, its volume increases since water expands as it gets warmer. Melting ice sheets also cause sea levels to rise, threatening coastal and island communities.

Loss of species

Climate change poses risks to the survival of species on both land and in the ocean. These risks increase as temperatures climb. Exacerbated by climate change, the world is losing species at a drastic rate 1,000 times greater than at any other time in recorded human history. One million species are at risk of becoming extinct within the next few decades. Forest fires, extreme weather, and invasive pests and diseases are among many threats related to climate change.

What are the solutions to climate change?

Climate change is already an urgent threat to millions of lives – but there are solutions and interventions that we can do in order for us to lessen or prevent the catastrophe from happening. From changing how we get our energy to limiting deforestation, here are some of the key solutions to climate change.

The main ways to stop climate change are to pressure government and business to:

- Keep fossil fuels in the ground and minimize the usage. Fossil fuels include coal; oil and gas, and the more that are extracted and burned, the worse climate change will get. All countries need to move their economies away from fossil fuels as soon as possible.

- Invest in renewable energy. Changing our main energy sources to clean and renewable energy is the best way to stop using fossil fuels. Using renewable energy not only helps and saves us natural energy, but also helps the nature to be managed. These includes the use of technologies like solar, wind, wave, tidal and geothermal power.

- Switch to sustainable transport. Petrol and diesel vehicles, planes and ships use fossil fuels that emits carbon emissions. Reducing car use, and switching to electric vehicles and minimizing plane travel will not only help stop climate change, it will reduce air pollution too.

- Restore nature to absorb more carbon. The natural world is very good at cleaning up our emissions, but we need to look after it as well. Planting trees in the right places or giving land back to nature through ‘rewilding’ schemes is a good place to start. This is because photosynthesizing plants draw down carbon dioxide as they grow, locking it away in soils.

- Protect forests like the Amazon. Forests are crucial in the fight against climate change, and protecting them is an important climate solution. Cutting down forests on an industrial scale destroys giant trees which could be sucking up huge amounts of carbon.

- -Protect the oceans. Oceans also absorb large amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, which helps to keep our climate stable. But many are overfished, used for oil and gas drilling or threatened by deep sea mining. Protecting oceans and the life in them is ultimately a way to protect ourselves from climate change.

- Reduce plastic. Plastic is made from oil, and the process of extracting, refining and turning oil into plastic (or even polyester, for clothing) is surprisingly carbon-intense. It doesn’t break down quickly in nature so a lot of plastic is burned, which contributes to emissions.

Suggestions

Plant more trees.

As a result of human agricultural expansion, nearly one-third of our Earth's forests have been destroyed; that can be compared to twice the size of the United States. Therefore, a momentary action is a prerequisite, as there are almost 10 countries that retain 70% of our world's remaining forests and nature.

Trees are our #1 climate change solution; they provide filtering our waste through absorbing and sequestering carbon via photosynthesis. Trees store the carbon dioxide from the air and soil and that emits oxygen through the Earth's atmosphere. They offer the things humans need, such as stabilizing our world's temperature, releasing oxygen for us to breathe, and a lot more.

Furthermore, it is preferable for the community to collaborate in the regeneration of damaged trees or vegetation rather than plant seedlings on inappropriate ground, which can harm our environment. As a result, the trees' root systems have already developed themselves, making this more efficient and cost-effective.

Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.

The use of raw materials, whether organic or inorganic, is a major contributor to greenhouse gas emissions worldwide. Many of the current climate change challenges could be significantly mitigated and avert it by implementing the three R's: reduce, reuse, and recycle.

It is well known that limiting the use of raw materials is one of the most effective ways or preventions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. According to Project Drawdown, recycling alone could reduce carbon dioxide emissions by 5.5–6.02 gigatons, which is equivalent to taking billions of cars off the road for an entire year.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is the Law of Global Catastrophe, and how does it affect climate change?

- The Law of Global Catastrophe refers to the potential damage or risk to human society, such as climate change, which has increasingly catastrophic consequences for human societies and natural ecosystems.

- This law addresses the potential effects on the globe, such as extreme weather, rising sea levels, resource depletion, and biodiversity loss, and emphasizes the necessity for comprehensive efforts to minimize or reduce the risk and adapt to the difficulties.

2. How can individuals contribute to mitigating the effects of climate change under the Law of Global Catastrophe?

- Individual can contribute simply by adapting the sustainable practices in their daily lives, such a setting their mindset like how to reduce energy consumption, minimizing waste, coordinate renewable energy and simply how proper waste, organize, renewable resources.

- Additionally, it could also contribute by advocating and supporting policies that address climate change and also by participating in different communities to promote environmental sustainability and educate other individuals regarding the impacts of climate change and to grow the cooperation and percentage to save the environment.

3. What are the key strategies for governments and organizations to address the risks highlighted by the Law of Global Catastrophe?

- Governments and organizations may play an important role in addressing global climate concerns by establishing strong climate policies, investing in resilient infrastructure, and encouraging international cooperation.

- Key initiatives include improving disaster preparedness, limiting greenhouse gas emissions, and, most crucially, investing in research and innovation to develop sustainable technologies and practices.
